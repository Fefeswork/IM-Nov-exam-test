Blog 3: What is Interaction for Data-visualization

Interaction refers to an action in which two or more people or objects influence one another. It also refers to communication or direct involvement with something or someone. Data visualization can be defined as the representation of information using charts, images graphs, diagrams, etc. In a previous article, Overview of Data Visualisation by Qi Li, Li gives us an overview of what the term ‘data’ means. She also provides an overview of what data visualisation is and discusses the uses for data visualisation in the context of web development. We further learn the types of data visualisation and the benefits of data visualisation, which is to understand the data that you’re presenting to the user/ audience. (Li, 2020: 21). Now that we have a clear definition of what ‘interaction’ and ‘data visualisation’ mean we now look into what interaction for data visualisation is and what it entails by referring to the article What is Interaction for Data Visualisation? by Evanthia Dimara and Charles Perin. In their article, they describe interaction as fundamental to data visualisation (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 2). We are introduced to a new term HCI- Human-Computer Interaction. They refer to an HCI review which summarises concepts that describe the causal relationships between humans and computer visualisation (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 2), which is what the article explores. The aim of the article is to explore what is meant by interaction for visualisation as it is seen as a broad topic. In section 3 of the article, Dimara and Perin begin to discuss the definitions of interaction for visualisation. “Interaction has been identified as an overloaded [92] and elusive [36,74] term, and it is challenging to find a solid definition of interaction” (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 3). This statement has some truth in it as it can sometimes be difficult, and it also depends on the context in which you speak of interaction. With referral to 32 papers with a definition tag in an attempt to define interaction implicitly and explicitly, these are some of the findings from these papers; “the papers reveal a mandatory component of interactions namely: external and internal entities, external and internal action” (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 3). Under the external entities, the entities describe are the user and the data where the user is a human. These two things exist co-independently without factoring in an interaction between them. Under external actions, interaction is described as a dialogue between the user and the visualisation system made of action-reaction pairs (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 4). It is understood that one thing/component would commit the action and receive a reaction from the other. In this case, it would be the user performing the action or input on the visualisation system. In terms of internal actions, this refers to the interaction with a visualization system that involves a cognitive act of the user, in essence a form of reasoning and analysis process on the data. In subsection 3 (3.1.2) the article looks further into what interaction for visualisation can involve. These are the following things that interaction for visualisation can involve: external physical objects such as a mouse, keyboard or pen. It may also include body movements, speech or eye movement. It can involve users’ actions which can change the data, can create new data representations (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 4). “Interaction for visualization is characterized as a goal-oriented activity that contains semantics that is sequential, incremental, and iterative” (Dimara and Perin, 2020: 5), by this, it is mean that when interacting with a visualisation system there is a goal that is needed to be achieved whether it’s changing the data presented incrementally or sequentially, this, in turn, means that the visualisation becomes iterative due to the interaction. Moving onto the HCI view on interaction, it extracts seven concepts of interaction these being Dialogue- this is seen as interaction as a cycle of communication through channelled input and output from a computer, think of it as having a conversation with someone, you bring up an atopic and converse over the said topic. Transmission- this is seen as the transmission of information between the computer and the user. Control- this type of interaction is seen as the minimisation of error, with control the interactions are continuous and are defined through a system that has inputs, outputs, feedback etc. Tool use- the tools that are used to allow users the ability to interact with the computer. Optimal behaviour, adaptive behaviour, enables the user or the visualisation system to adapt to the changes the input brings forth., embodiment and experience. Reflecting on the new information I’ve learned; the article has provided many outlooks on what interaction for data visualisation is. The article tackled mainly interaction and different types of interaction but in turn, related it back to how it affects data visualisation. I thought of a few examples while reading the article, these examples involve cases in which the input/ action of the user affects the way in which data is visualised: an example would be a quiz that displays a different result/set of data based on how you’ve answered the questions or data displaying differently based on the settings you’ve chosen.
